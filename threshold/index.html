Recovery

The comedown & the "Tuesday blues"

Why the crash often skips a day, what's happening in your brain, and which recovery strategies actually have evidence behind them.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 11 min read
✓ Medically reviewed by
Dr. A. Reyes, MD - addiction medicine

In this guide

· Why the comedown skips a day · What's happening in your brain · What actually helps (graded by evidence) · The supplement question · When it's more than a comedown

If you've ever felt fine the day after MDMA and then been flattened on the second day, you're not imagining it. Regular users call it "Suicide Tuesday" - the low that lands a day or two after a Saturday night. It's one of the most-searched things about MDMA, and one of the most poorly explained. Here's the honest version.

Why the comedown skips a day

MDMA works largely by flooding your brain with serotonin. That flood is the good part on the night. The problem is what comes after: your brain has temporarily emptied its serotonin stores and needs time to rebuild them. The dip is usually deepest 24 to 72 hours later - which is why the hangover seems to "skip" the next morning and hit on the Tuesday.

⚠ The single biggest comedown driver

Redosing. Taking more during the night to chase the peak deepens serotonin depletion and reliably makes the after-effects worse and longer. The night feels better; the week feels worse.

What's happening in your brain

With a single moderate dose, most people are through the worst of it within one to three days. After heavy or repeated dosing - several pills, or a whole weekend - low energy, foggy thinking, and low mood can stretch to five to seven days as serotonin stores stay chronically depleted. The dip is biphasic: a drop, a brief lift, then a deeper low around day five.

What actually helps

This is where most articles start making things up. Here's what the evidence actually supports, graded honestly:

Strong evidenceSleep, time, and not redosing.

Nothing beats rest and recovery time. The serotonin system rebuilds itself; your main job is not to interfere with it and not to stack another session on top.

Strong evidenceHydration with electrolytes - but not too much.

Aim for roughly 200-500 ml of fluid per hour while active, with electrolytes. Drinking too much plain water is its own danger (hyponatremia) - more is not better.

Mixed / emergingNutrition and social connection.

Foods with omega-3s, antioxidants, and tryptophan (oily fish, nuts, berries, leafy greens) are sensible. Being around supportive people genuinely seems to ease the low - isolation makes it worse.

✓ Myth vs. fact

Myth: "5-HTP right after rolling refills your serotonin and prevents the crash."
Fact: Taking serotonin-boosting supplements too soon can risk serotonin syndrome - a genuinely dangerous condition. Timing is everything, and this isn't a same-night fix.

The supplement question

There's a whole "pre-load and post-load" supplement culture (5-HTP, NAC, magnesium, vitamin C, omega-3s). Some of it is reasonable; much of it is oversold. The honest state of the evidence:

  • 5-HTP - animal studies suggest it may reduce serotonergic damage, but it must be timed carefully (typically started a couple of days after, not during) to avoid serotonin syndrome. Animal studies
  • NAC / antioxidants - plausible mechanism (mopping up free radicals), but human clinical evidence is thin. Animal studies
  • Magnesium - may ease jaw-clenching during use; not a recovery cure. Mixed / emerging

For the full ingredient-by-ingredient breakdown, timed across the experience and graded by evidence, see our supplements page.

🚨 When it's more than a comedown

A comedown lifts within a week. If low mood, anxiety, sleep problems, or cravings persist beyond one to two weeks, that may be emerging depression, an anxiety disorder, or a sign of problematic use - not just serotonin recovery. That's a moment to see a GP or mental-health professional, not to wait it out.

The comedown is real, it's chemical, and it's mostly temporary. Treat the after-effects with the same seriousness as the night itself, don't redose to outrun it, and know the line where "rough week" becomes "get support."

The science

How MDMA works in the body

From the first dose to full recovery: what actually happens, hour by hour and day by day, with the evidence behind each stage.

MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine) is a serotonin-releasing empathogen with stimulant properties. Its arc runs on two clocks: the hours of the experience itself, and the days to weeks your brain then spends rebuilding. Here is both, honestly, with sources and an evidence grade on each claim.

The experience, hour by hour

0 to 8 hours · Strong evidence

Taken orally, MDMA comes on in about 30 to 45 minutes. Effects peak around 60 to 90 minutes in (plasma levels peak, Tmax, near 2 hours) and then ease off. A single dose lasts roughly 3 to 6 hours; a half-dose top-up extends that to about 5 to 8. Its half-life is long, around 7 to 9 hours, which means the drug is still being cleared well into the next day, even after the felt effects are gone.

In the brain: the serotonin flood

During the peak · Strong evidence

MDMA's signature comes from how it hijacks the serotonin system. It has the highest affinity for the serotonin transporter (SERT), with about ten times less for the dopamine (DAT) and norepinephrine (NET) transporters. After entering a serotonin neuron through SERT, it makes the vesicle transporter (VMAT2) dump stored serotonin into the cell, then runs SERT in reverse, so serotonin pours out into the synapse instead of being pulled back in. Dopamine and norepinephrine are released too. That flood of serotonin is the warmth, openness, and energy of the peak.

The load: heat and oxidative stress

During & just after · Mixed / emerging Animal studies

The same surge that creates the experience also puts the brain under strain. As MDMA and its by-products are broken down, reactive quinone metabolites can redox-cycle into reactive oxygen species (free radicals), and body heat (hyperthermia) is one of the biggest drivers of that free-radical load. How much strain the brain takes is shaped by dose, frequency, overheating, dehydration, and setting. This oxidative mechanism is why antioxidants and serotonin precursors are studied for neuroprotection, though most of that protective evidence is still at the animal stage and should not be read as a green light.

🚨 What multiplies the load

Heat, redosing, dehydration, and frequent sessions stack on top of each other. A hot room and a redose are not separate risks; together they drive far more oxidative strain than either alone.

Sources: Capela et al., Mechanisms of MDMA-induced oxidative stress and organ damage (2010).

The days after: depletion and the midweek low

Days 1 to 7 · Mixed / emerging

Emptying the tank has a cost. A single recreational dose can lower serotonin stores by roughly 50 to 80 percent within hours. The brain then begins resynthesising serotonin over about 3 to 7 days. The pattern is biphasic: an initial drop, a brief partial recovery a day or two later, and then a second, deeper low around days 5 to 7 - the "Tuesday" low people describe after a weekend.

Full recovery: weeks, not days

Weeks 1 to 6 · Mixed / emerging

Beyond the felt low, the system keeps rebuilding under the surface. Full normalisation of transporter density and receptor sensitivity is estimated at about 2 to 6 weeks after a single moderate dose. Heavy or repeated use lengthens this and can vary by brain region. Importantly, the evidence for lasting, selective serotonergic harm in humans is not conclusive, and that uncertainty is part of the honest picture. What reliably makes recovery slower is clear, though: higher doses, redosing, frequent use, and overheating.

Supplements

Supplements: what helps, honestly

No pill makes MDMA safe. But there's a real, evidence-graded rationale for supporting the body across the experience - if you're honest about what the science does and doesn't show.

This is one of the most-searched and worst-answered questions in the space. The honest version: a lot of the popular "pre-load / post-load" advice is oversold, much of the neuroprotection evidence is animal-stage, and timing some of it wrong can be dangerous. A few things genuinely matter (electrolytes, sleep), and several others are plausible but unproven in humans. Below, every ingredient carries an evidence grade so you can see exactly how solid the ground is.

⚠ The one principle worth keeping

Support is sequenced, not single-dose. The body faces different demands before, during, at sleep, and the day after, so anything useful is timed to the moment, not swallowed all at once. That's the logic the four phases below follow.

🚨 Read this before you load up on anything

Supplements do not make MDMA safe, and they don't undo a bad dose, a bad mix, or overheating. Timing is not optional: serotonin precursors like tryptophan or 5-HTP taken during or too soon after can trigger serotonin syndrome, which is dangerous. If you take any medication, especially an SSRI, SNRI, or MAOI, check interactions with a doctor or pharmacist first.

MDMA 101: what it is and what to expect

The plain version: what MDMA actually is, how a typical experience unfolds, and the questions worth asking yourself before you ever consider it.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
✓ Medically reviewed

What it is

MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine) is a synthetic compound that is part stimulant, part empathogen. It makes most people feel energetic, warm, emotionally open, and connected to others. "Molly" usually refers to MDMA sold as powder or crystal; "ecstasy" usually refers to pressed pills. The names get used interchangeably, but they are not a guarantee of what is inside, which is the whole problem.

⚠ "Molly" is not always MDMA

Pills and powders sold as MDMA regularly contain other substances, the wrong dose, or nothing useful at all. This is exactly why testing your supply is the single most protective habit there is.

What a typical experience looks like

Taken orally, MDMA comes on in about 30-45 minutes, peaks around 1.5-2 hours, and the main effects last roughly 3-6 hours. There is a come-up, a plateau, and then a gradual offset. For the hour-by-hour breakdown of what is happening in your brain and body, see how it works in the body.

Questions worth asking first

  • Am I on any medication, especially an antidepressant? Some combinations are dangerous. See what not to mix.
  • Do I have a heart condition, or a personal or family history of serious mental illness? These raise the risk meaningfully.
  • Can I test what I have, control my dose, and be somewhere safe with people I trust?
  • Am I in a good headspace? Mindset and setting shape the experience more than almost anything else.

Before you use

Dosing & redosing

How much is too much, why body weight matters, and why the second dose is the one that wrecks your week.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 8 min read
✓ Medically reviewed

Dose by body weight

A common harm-reduction reference point is roughly 1-1.5 mg per kg of body weight, which for most people lands under about 120 mg in a session. Higher doses do not make the experience proportionally better; they mostly increase the risk of overheating, a harder comedown, and lasting after-effects.

⚠ Pills are stronger than people think

European drug agencies report that high-strength MDMA is now standard, and at some festivals over half of tablets tested contained around 200 mg, well above a typical dose. A whole pill is not a reliable unit. Test and weigh rather than guessing.

The test-dose habit

Take a small fraction first (for example a quarter), then wait. Effects can take 30-60 minutes to arrive, and the urge to "top up" before they do is how people accidentally take far too much.

Redosing: the single biggest comedown driver

If you redose, keep it to at most one small top-up, taken within the first hour or two. Chasing the peak deepens serotonin depletion and reliably makes the after-effects worse and longer. The night feels better; the week feels worse. The science of why is in how it works in the body.

Expert consensusLess, tested, and spaced out.

Lower doses, a tested supply, and not redosing are the three levers that do the most to keep a session safer.

Information, not medical advice. Figures are harm-reduction reference points, not a prescription or an endorsement of use.

Testing your supply

Reagent kits, fentanyl strips, what each one can and cannot tell you, and the one thing no test can do.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
✓ Medically reviewed

Why test at all

What is sold as MDMA is often not, or not only, MDMA. Drug-checking at events keeps finding adulterants and wildly variable strength. In 2025 festival testing, fentanyl turned up in a meaningful share of "MDMA" samples. Testing will not make a drug safe, but it can stop you taking the wrong thing entirely.

Reagent kits vs. fentanyl strips

They do different jobs, and you want both:

  • Reagent kits use colour changes to suggest what a substance likely is. They hint at presence, not purity or dose. Compare the reaction against the kit's chart.
  • Fentanyl test strips check specifically for fentanyl and some analogues. You dissolve a little of your substance in water and dip the strip.

🚨 No reagent detects fentanyl

Do not trust any liquid reagent that claims to find fentanyl. Use dedicated fentanyl test strips, and remember that even a clean test is not a guarantee, contamination can be uneven within the same batch.

Where to get checked

Lab-grade drug-checking services (at some festivals and clinics) catch far more than home kits can. Our Resources page points to where to find them.

Information, not medical advice. Testing reduces some risks; it does not make any drug safe.

Safety-critical

What not to mix

The medication and drug combinations that genuinely matter, ranked by how dangerous they actually are.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 9 min read
✓ Medically reviewed

If you read one guide before using, make it this one. Most serious, avoidable harm comes from combinations, not from MDMA alone.

MAOIs: the most dangerous

Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (some older antidepressants, and a few other drugs) combined with MDMA can cause life-threatening serotonin toxicity or a hypertensive crisis. MAOIs can linger in the body for up to about two weeks after the last dose, so the danger window is long.

Strong evidenceNever combine MDMA with an MAOI.

SSRIs & SNRIs: two problems at once

These common antidepressants both blunt MDMA's effects and raise the risk of serotonin syndrome. The danger is that people, feeling little, take more to compensate, which is exactly the wrong move.

Other combinations to avoid

  • Tramadol lowers the seizure threshold and adds serotonin load.
  • Other stimulants (cocaine, amphetamine, high-dose caffeine) stack cardiovascular and overheating risk.
  • Alcohol worsens dehydration and clouds judgment about dosing and heat.

🚨 Watch for serotonin syndrome

Agitation, confusion, rapid heartbeat, high temperature, muscle rigidity, or twitching after mixing serotonergic drugs is a medical emergency. Call emergency services and say what was taken. See spot it & respond.

Information, not medical advice. If you take any prescription medication, check with a doctor or pharmacist. This is not an endorsement of use.

Festival & retreat

Set & setting

"Set" is your mindset, "setting" is where and with whom. Together they shape an experience more than the dose does.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
✓ Reviewed

Set: your mindset going in

How you feel beforehand tends to get amplified. Going in anxious, sleep-deprived, or to escape something difficult raises the odds of a hard time. A settled, willing headspace and a rough intention for the experience help a lot.

Setting: where and with whom

Safety comes from people you trust, a way to get somewhere calm if you need it, and not being alone. The risks differ by place:

  • At a festival: plan for heat and crowds, agree a meet-up point, keep water and electrolytes, and know where the welfare or medical tent is.
  • At a retreat: vet the facilitator, ask how they handle a medical or psychological emergency, and plan your integration before you begin.
  • At home: a trusted, sober-enough sitter makes a real difference. See being a sitter.

⚠ Heat is the quiet danger

Most festival emergencies involve overheating or, less often, drinking far too much water. Take breaks, sip steadily, and check on each other.

Information, not medical advice. This is harm-reduction guidance, not an endorsement of use.

Emergencies

Spot it & respond

How to tell a hard moment from a real emergency, what to do, how to be a sitter, and why delay is the thing that actually kills.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
✓ Medically reviewed

🚨 Call emergency services now if someone is:

Overheating or very hot to the touch, confused or disoriented, having a seizure, vomiting repeatedly, becoming rigid or twitching, or will not wake up. Tell medics exactly what was taken. You will not get them into more trouble by being honest, and delay is the single biggest factor in fatal outcomes.

The three to know

  • Overheating (hyperthermia): very hot, not sweating, confused. Move to a cool place, remove layers, cool the body, get help.
  • Hyponatremia (too much water): headache, nausea, confusion after drinking large amounts of plain water. More water makes it worse. Get help.
  • Serotonin syndrome: agitation, high temperature, rapid heartbeat, muscle rigidity or twitching, especially after mixing. Emergency.

Being a sitter

If you are looking after someone, your job is calm presence: stay with them, keep them cool and hydrated (sips, not floods), reduce stimulation, and reassure. Most hard moments pass with a quiet space and a steady voice. Know the line above, and do not hesitate to call for help if you cross it.

Information, not medical advice. When in doubt, call your local emergency number.

Integration & getting help

Making sense of what came up, working with a hard experience, persistent effects, and knowing when to reach for support.

By the Threshold editorial team
Updated June 2026 · 7 min read
✓ Reviewed

What integration means

Integration is the unglamorous part where an experience actually becomes useful: giving yourself time, reflecting (journalling, talking, rest), and carrying any insight into ordinary life rather than chasing the next session. The days after are part of the experience, not an afterthought, see the comedown & recovery.

When it was hard

Difficult experiences are common and not the same as harm. Talking it through with someone you trust, or an integration-aware therapist, helps most. Be gentle with yourself and avoid big decisions while you are still raw.

🚨 When to seek professional help

If low mood, anxiety, sleep problems, or cravings last beyond one to two weeks, or if you have lasting visual disturbances (HPPD), distressing intrusive thoughts, or you feel detached from reality, that is a reason to see a GP or mental-health professional. Persistent adverse effects are under-researched and deserve real support, not waiting it out.

Where to find support

Our Resources page lists confidential helplines and integration-aware practitioners.

Information, not medical advice. This topic can be heavy. If you are struggling, you deserve real support, and it is okay to ask for it.
